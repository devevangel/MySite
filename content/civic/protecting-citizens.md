## One farmer. One agent. Five thousand students.

In 2020, a 27-year-old American farmer named Philip Walton was kidnapped near the Niger-Nigeria border. Within 96 hours, Navy SEAL Team 6 flew into Northern Nigeria, tracked the kidnappers' phones, and extracted him alive [1]. Secretary of State Pompeo said: *"We will never abandon any American taken hostage."*

Go back further. In 1985, cartel members murdered DEA agent Kiki Camarena in Mexico. America shut down the entire US-Mexico border, launched its largest homicide investigation, and spent forty years hunting every leader involved. No cartel has deliberately killed a DEA agent since.

One agent. One nation's full wrath. Forty years of pursuit.

## Sudan, 2023

When civil war trapped foreign nationals in Khartoum, the contrast became impossible to ignore.

America deployed special operations troops in MH-47 helicopters — in and out in under an hour, embassy staff airlifted, then armed drones escorted a 500-mile bus convoy to safety [2]. India launched Operation Kaveri, flying IAF C-130J aircraft with armed Garud commandos who kept engines running on the tarmac while evacuating nearly 3,862 citizens [3]. Germany moved 740 people in two days.

**Nigeria?** Fifteen of forty required buses. Nigerians stranded in the desert when fuel ran out. Payment delays from the Central Bank. Visa fees demanded by Egypt. Students walking through conflict zones to reach evacuation points [4].

No military aircraft. No armed escort. Rented buses.

## And then — home

The pattern doesn't stop at the border. In November 2025, 25 schoolgirls were kidnapped in Kebbi. Three days later, 303 students and 12 teachers were taken in Niger state [5]. More than 1,680 students have been abducted since Chibok in 2014.

Between July 2024 and June 2025 alone, 4,722 people were kidnapped across Nigeria, with at least 762 killed [6]. Nigerians paid ₦2.23 trillion in ransom between May 2023 and April 2024 [7].

344 boys in Katsina. 279 girls in Zamfara. 150 in Kaduna. Over 200 more in Kaduna. 303 in Niger state. Hundreds of children dragged into the bush — again, and again, and again.

## The message inaction sends

America sent SEAL Team 6 across international borders for **one** farmer. India flew armed commandos into a warzone for its students. Germany evacuated 740 people in **two days**.

Nigeria has watched thousands of its children taken from classrooms into forests, year after year, and the pattern repeats.

Inaction is a message. Silence is a message. And the message Nigerians receive is clear: *you are on your own.*

When a government protects its people with everything it has, it builds something you cannot buy — **patriotism**. Citizens who know their nation will come for them will fight for that nation, build for it, bleed for it.

When a government abandons its people, it kills something no policy can resurrect: **belief**.

## What Nigerians deserve

A government that makes the world understand one thing:

**You do not touch our people.**

Not one. Not ever.

This is not a call for foreign military intervention. It is a demand for a Nigerian state that treats the safety of every citizen — in Sudan, in Kebbi, in Zamfara — with the same urgency other nations treat one farmer or one agent.

Protection is not charity. It is the first obligation of governance.
